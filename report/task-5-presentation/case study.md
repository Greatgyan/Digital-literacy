Case Study: The "Urgent Account Verification" Phishing Attack
What the Crime Is
Phishing is a type of social engineering cybercrime where an attacker masquerades as a trusted entity—such as a bank, university, or popular service provider—to deceive victims into revealing sensitive information like login credentials, credit card numbers, or personal identification.

Step-by-Step Execution
Preparation: The attacker creates a spoofed email that looks identical to an official "Security Alert" from a major bank. They also host a fraudulent login page that mirrors the bank's actual portal.

The Hook: The attacker sends a mass email to thousands of recipients with an urgent subject line: "Action Required: Unusual login attempt detected on your account."

The Deception: The victim, feeling pressured, clicks the "Secure My Account" link in the email. This redirects them to the fake website rather than the official banking site.

The Theft: The victim enters their username and password. The attacker captures these credentials in real-time. Often, the fake site then asks for a one-time password (OTP) to bypass two-factor authentication.

Exfiltration: With the credentials, the attacker logs into the real bank account, changes the contact information to lock the victim out, and drains the funds.

Target Audience & Consequences
While anyone can be targeted, attackers often focus on students or the elderly, who may be less familiar with URL verification. The consequences are severe: immediate financial loss, potential identity theft if personal data is sold on the dark web, and significant emotional distress and time spent recovering compromised accounts.
